01 — About

Why I build things

I am a student developer interested in the intersection between software, hardware, and creative problem-solving. I enjoy taking abstract ideas and transforming them into working applications, whether that means designing a web interface, programming algorithms, or experimenting with physical computing.

Right now, I split my time between writing clean code in Python and JavaScript, experimenting with digital logic circuits, and working on multidisciplinary projects that connect technology with practical problem-solving. These experiences have taught me how software, hardware, and creativity can work together to transform ideas into functional solutions.

Outside of my own projects, community leadership is a huge part of what I do. Tech can sometimes feel a bit uninviting or heavily male-dominated, so being named the first female Coding Club President at my school was a really meaningful milestone for me. It strengthened my passion for mentoring others and helping them overcome the initial intimidation they may feel. I love teaching what I know, working with collaborative teams under tight deadlines, and making sure our club is a welcoming, inclusive space where anyone, especially other girls trying out tech for the first time, can pull up a chair and start building.

04 — Selected Works

Projects

🏆 2nd Place · International

Web App · Stripe Competition

RideRush

Theme Park Operations Simulator

RideRush is a data-driven web application that uses live JavaScript logic to simulate real-time theme park operations, visitor capacity, and financial metrics. I wanted to challenge myself by building something that required complex state management and algorithmic thinking. The app processes data dynamically and features an integrated CSV parser to handle mock operational stats. Earning 2nd place internationally in the Stripe Competition was an amazing milestone that pushed my understanding of software development and web programming.

JavaScript (ES6+) HTML5 Canvas CSS Grid CSV Parser Engine

My Learning

Developing RideRush challenged me to think beyond basic programming. I had to manage changing data, user interactions, and complex logic while making the application easy to understand. Through debugging and redesigning features, I learned how important organization and planning are when building larger software projects.

🏆 3rd Place · Fraser Hacks

Hackathon · John Fraser S.S.

MED+Search

Emergency Resource Locator

MED+Search is an application designed to help users organize and locate important medical resources during time-sensitive situations. I developed this with my team during a weekend hackathon. We wanted to create a clean, fast interface that removes confusion when people need clear data the most. Placing 3rd at Fraser Hacks taught me an immense amount about rapid prototyping, managing development lifecycles under a strict clock, and designing clean user workflows.

UI Mockups Logic Arrays Frontend Layouts

My Learning

One of the biggest challenges during the hackathon was balancing speed with quality. With limited time, our team had to quickly decide which features created the most value for users. This experience showed me the importance of teamwork, prioritization, and designing technology around real human needs.

Physical Computing

Embedded Systems & Robotics Portfolio

Arduino & Tinkercad Circuit Collection

This is a documented collection of my robotics and hardware projects created using Arduino and Tinkercad. My layouts include multi-LED traffic light systems and environmental sensor arrays. For these projects, I focused on reading data from analog inputs (specifically A0 and A1) to trigger distinct digital outputs. It's given me a great foundation in physical computing and understanding how software interacts directly with physical hardware components.

Arduino Firmware Tinkercad Circuits Analog/Digital Sensors

My Learning

Working with hardware changed the way I view programming. Instead of seeing code as only digital instructions, I learned how software can directly influence the physical world through sensors, circuits, and motors.

05 — Leadership

Leadership & extracurriculars

💻

Coding Club

President

I run our weekly workshops and design the introductory programming curriculum for our 21 active members. My goal is to make coding approachable and fun for students who have never written code before, teaching variables, loops, and basic algorithms while keeping a team organized and motivated.

Becoming Coding Club President taught me that leadership is not only about knowing the most technical information. It is about making complicated ideas understandable, encouraging others to participate, and creating an environment where beginners feel confident learning something new.

🩺

MedLife

Fundraising Executive

As Fundraising Executive, I help plan and run events that raise money for medical and educational resources in underprivileged communities. One of my favorite projects was organizing a churro fundraiser and bake sale from scratch, planning the menu and pricing, booking our table, making posters, and handling sales during the school day. It was a lot of on-the-spot problem solving (churros are messier to sell than you'd think), but we ended up raising over $200, which felt like real proof that a small, well-organized event can add up to something that actually helps people.

🤖

School Robotics Team

Team Member · FIRST Robotics Competition

I'm part of our school's FIRST Robotics Competition (FRC) team, where we design, build, and program a full-scale competition robot every season on tight competition deadlines. I work mainly with the programming subteam, and it's honestly become one of my favorite parts of high school. There's something really satisfying about writing code that makes a 120-pound robot actually move, turn, and complete a task the way you intended. It's also taught me a different kind of teamwork than a typical school project: when a match starts in a few minutes and the robot isn't responding the way it should, you learn fast how to communicate clearly, trust your teammates' strengths, and stay calm under pressure instead of panicking.

✏️

CAD Club

Marketing Executive

As Marketing Executive for CAD Club, I get to combine my interest in design with the more creative, people-facing side of STEM. I plan and run outreach campaigns to get students excited about our CAD workshops, which means everything from designing posters and social posts to figuring out how to actually get people to show up. I really enjoy this role because it's a reminder that promoting technology matters just as much as building it. A great workshop doesn't do much good if nobody knows about it, so making our projects and events look appealing and approachable has become something I genuinely care about.

07 — Reflections

“

What technology has taught me

Through programming, robotics, and leadership experiences, I have learned that creating technology is a process of continuous improvement. My first solutions are rarely my final solutions, and that used to frustrate me before I understood it was actually the whole point. Every bug, failed prototype, and difficult challenge has taught me how to think more creatively and approach problems with patience instead of giving up the moment something doesn't work.

Leading Coding Club and working within teams like FRC also showed me that technology is never really a solo effort. The best solutions come from asking questions, listening to people with different perspectives, and being willing to start over when an idea doesn't pan out. Looking back at everything I've built, broken, and rebuilt, I don't think I'm the same student who first got curious about how apps and circuits worked. I'm more patient, more collaborative, and a lot more comfortable not having the answer right away, and I think that mindset matters just as much as any line of code I've written.

Inaaya Khan
